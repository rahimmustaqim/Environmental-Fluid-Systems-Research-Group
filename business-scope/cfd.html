Why this matters

CFD and Environmental Fluid Dynamics

Computational Fluid Dynamics and environmental fluid dynamics are used to investigate complex flow behaviour that cannot be fully understood through simple calculations alone. This includes turbulence, dispersion, mixing, wake formation, flow-structure interaction, and detailed local flow processes.

Main purpose: to provide detailed flow simulation and visualization for engineering, environmental, hydraulic, coastal, and industrial applications where local flow behaviour is important.

Key Questions Addressed

  • How does fluid move around structures or within complex geometries?
  • Where do turbulence, recirculation, or high-velocity zones occur?
  • How are pollutants, sediment, heat, or particles dispersed by the flow?
  • How can design alternatives improve hydraulic or environmental performance?

Typical Analysis

Flow and Turbulence Simulation

Detailed simulation of velocity, pressure, turbulence, vortices, recirculation zones, and flow interaction with structures.

Dispersion and Environmental Analysis

Assessment of contaminant dispersion, sediment movement, particle transport, mixing, and environmental flow processes.

Why It Is Important

CFD provides detailed insight into local flow phenomena that are often difficult to measure directly. It can reveal critical zones of high velocity, pressure concentration, turbulence, erosion potential, or poor mixing.

This analysis supports better engineering design by allowing alternatives to be tested virtually before construction or implementation.

Expected Outputs

  • Velocity, pressure, and turbulence distribution.
  • Flow pathlines, streamlines, and visualization outputs.
  • Dispersion, mixing, or particle transport results.
  • Comparison of design alternatives.
  • Technical recommendations for improving hydraulic or environmental performance.

Application Areas

This service can be applied to hydraulic structures, coastal and offshore structures, environmental dispersion studies, industrial flow systems, urban wind or ventilation studies, sediment transport, and fluid-structure interaction problems.
